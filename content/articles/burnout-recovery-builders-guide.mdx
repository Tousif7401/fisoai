You used to wake up excited to code. Now? Even opening your laptop feels heavy.

Projects that once sparked joy now feel like obligations. The curiosity that drove you to learn has been replaced by numbness.

**This is burnout — and it's not your fault.**

## Recognizing Burnout

Burnout doesn't always look like giving up. Sometimes it looks like:

- **Grinding harder** while getting less done
- **Irritability** at things that never bothered you before
- **Physical exhaustion** no amount of sleep fixes
- **Detachment** from work you used to care about

If this sounds familiar, listen: **you're not broken.** You're human, and you've been running on empty.

## Why Builders Burn Out

Developers and creators face unique pressures:
- **The "always on" culture** — Slack, Discord, Twitter, never truly offline
- **Rapid change fatigue** — New frameworks, new tools, always something to learn
- **Side project pressure** — The idea that you *must* be building something on the side
- **Comparison trap** — Seeing others launch and feeling "behind"

These aren't character flaws. They're systemic issues, and recognizing them is step one.

## The First Step: Stop

It sounds counterintuitive, but **you can't hustle your way out of burnout.**

Taking a break isn't giving up. It's admitting you need recovery — like resting an injured muscle before running again.

Start small:
- Take one evening completely off (no coding Twitter, no side projects)
- Go one weekend without opening your laptop
- Set Slack to "away" and actually *be* away

## Reconnect with Why You Started

Before burnout, there was something that drew you to building:

- Was it the thrill of creating something from nothing?
- The satisfaction of solving a tricky problem?
- The joy of learning how things work?

Spend time doing that thing — **without expectations.** Build something tiny. Learn something useless. Just for the fun of it.

## Rebuild Energy, Not Motivation

Motivation is fickle. It comes and goes. **Energy is what you can actually manage.**

Protect your energy with:
- **Sleep consistency** — Same bedtime, even on weekends (your brain needs rhythm)
- **Movement** — Even a 10-minute walk resets your mental state
- **Nutrition** — Your brain can't focus on fumes
- **Social connection** — Isolation magnifies burnout

These aren't "productivity hacks." They're biology.

## Set Actual Boundaries

Burnout thrives when work and life blur together.

Try this:
- **Hard stop times** — "6pm is when I close my laptop, no exceptions"
- **No-code zones** — "My bedroom is for rest, not coding"
- **Notification discipline** — Turn off Slack on weekends. The world won't end.

Boundaries aren't weakness. They're sustainability.

## The Slow Return

When you're ready to code again, start small:

1. **Pick something fun** — Not the urgent project. Something you genuinely enjoy.
2. **Timebox it** — 30 minutes max. Leave while you still want more.
3. **Celebrate tiny wins** — Did you write one function? That's progress.

Rushing back to full intensity will just send you back into burnout. Let yourself ease in.

## When to Seek Help

If burnout is accompanied by:
- Persistent hopelessness
- Sleep that won't improve
- Thoughts of not wanting to exist

**Please reach out to a professional.** Burnout can overlap with depression, and both deserve proper support.

---

**You're not a machine.** You're a person who builds things — and people need rest, recovery, and care.

*This too shall pass. What felt impossible today will feel manageable again. Take it one small step at a time.*
